## The dark business model of "Free" Apps

There is a maxim in Silicon Valley that dictates: *"If you don't pay for the product, you are the product"*. And this phrase **has never been truer** than in the world of personal finance (Fintech).

Maintaining a highly expensive, ultra-secure server infrastructure connected to hundreds of banks via APIs costs millions a year. So, *how does an application survive financially that is apparently 100% free for you?*

**The answer is divided into two pillars:**

### 1. Referral commissions
They read and analyze your financial profile (how much you earn, if you are in the red at the end of the month) and bombard you with offers in the dashboard.
They offer you pre-approved loans with high interest rates, revolving credit cards, and insurance that supposedly "suits you". If you accept, they take a commission from the lender.

### 2. Data Brokerage
Even if the privacy policy assures you that *"your data is completely anonymous"*, the sum of metadata (where you buy, exactly how much you earn, what time you pay Netflix, when and where you refuel) is worth a great deal to digital marketing agencies.

> *"Handing over the keys to your entire transactional life to an anonymous third-party company, in exchange for seeing a colorful pie chart at the end of the month, is possibly one of the worst deals you can make online."*

## The great schism: "Unconscious Automation"

But in addition to the privacy problem, automatic banking apps hide **a significant psychological problem**.

By making everything fully automatic, **the awareness of the expense disappears**.
It is the automation of financial blindness.

When Mint or your Chase app scans your account at dawn, sees a $20 Amazon receipt and drops it into a box that says *"Shopping"*, your brain doesn't even notice the transaction. **You are delegating your cognitive responsibility to an algorithm**.

That is precisely why many users of these apps reach the end of the month just as broke as ever; the only difference is that now they see in an elegant chart at what exact moment they spent it all.

Real savings **are not generated by viewing charts after the fact**; they are generated by actively tracking the expense in the moment it occurs.
