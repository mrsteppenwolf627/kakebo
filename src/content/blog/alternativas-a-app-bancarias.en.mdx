## 🕵️‍♂️ The dark business model of "Free" Apps

There is a maxim in Silicon Valley that dictates: *"If you don't pay for the product, rest assured that you are the product"*. And believe us: this phrase **has never been truer** than in the wild west of personal finance (Fintech).

Maintaining a highly expensive, ultra-secure server infrastructure connected by brute force to hundreds of banks via private APIs costs on average thousands (or millions) a year. So, *how does an application survive financially that is apparently 100% free for you?*

**The unpleasant answer is divided into two pillars:**

### 1. Abusive referral commissions
They read and dissect your financial profile (how much you earn, if you are in the red at the end of the month, what car you drive) and **bombard you** relentlessly in the dashboard.
They offer you pre-approved loans with usurious interest rates, deadly *revolving* credit cards, and mobile or car insurance that supposedly "suits you". If you bite, they take a tremendous commission from lenders.

### 2. The obscene *Data Brokerage*
Even if the privacy policy assures actively and passively that *"your data is completely anonymous"*, the enormous sum of metadata (where you buy bread, exactly how much you earn, what time you pay Netflix, and when and where you refuel gas) is literally worth pure gold for digital marketing agencies.

> *"Handing over the master keys to your entire transactional life to an anonymous third-party company, in exchange for seeing a colorful circular bar chart at the end of the month, is possibly one of the worst deals you can accept on the internet."*

## 🤖 The great schism: "Unconscious Automation"

But wait, because in addition to the devastating privacy problem, automatic banking apps hide **a terribly painful psychological problem**.

By making absolutely everything one hundred percent automatic, **the awareness of the expense magically disappears**.
It is the automation of blindness.

When Mint or your Chase app scans your account at dawn, sees a $20 Amazon receipt and drags it without warning into a box that says *"Shopping"*, your human brain doesn't even notice the transaction. **You are delegating 100% of your cognitive responsibility to a blind code algorithm**.

That is precisely why the vast majority of users of these famous apps reach the dreaded 31st of the month just as broke as their peers; the only difference is that now they see in an elegant traffic light chart at what exact moment they accidentally spent it all.

Real savings **are not generated by viewing charts after the fact**; they are generated by viscerally feeling the analog friction of the expense right in the painful millisecond it occurs.
